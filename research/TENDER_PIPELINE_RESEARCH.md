## 1. Market Size & Significance

### Procurement as Share of Budget

- Public procurement through the budget (subvented organizations) accounts for approximately **40% of government spending** (IMF, 2018).
- The **2025/26 National Budget** is MK 8.1 trillion (~US$4.8 billion at current rates), up 24.3% from MK 6.5 trillion in 2024/25.
- That implies roughly **MK 3.2 trillion (~US$1.9 billion)** flows through public procurement annually.
- Government expenditure = ~31.1% of GDP (2025/26 budget).
- **73% of development project funding** comes from donors (2024/25 data).
- Development expenditure in 2025/26: MK 1.62 trillion, of which MK 1.31 trillion (81%) is from foreign resources and only MK 314 billion (19%) from domestic resources.

### Key Takeaway

The Malawi procurement market is large relative to the economy and heavily donor-funded. Any tender aggregation platform must cover both domestic government AND donor-funded opportunities to capture the full market.

## 2. Legal Framework & Key Legislation

### Evolution of the Law

| Year | Legislation | Status |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 2003 | Public Procurement Act (Chapter 37:03) | Superseded |
| 2017 | Public Procurement and Disposal of Public Assets Act | Superseded |
| 2025 | **Public Procurement and Disposal of Public Assets Act No. 7 of 2025** | **Current law** |

- The 2025 Act was signed January 31, 2025, and **came into force March 31, 2025**.
- It replaced the 2017 Act following a presidential directive to review procurement law.
- Dissemination campaigns ran May-June 2025 across all regions (Lilongwe, Liwonde, Blantyre, Mzuzu).

### The Regulatory Body: PPDA

- **Full name:** Public Procurement and Disposal of Assets Authority
- **Status:** Impartial and independent institution
- **Website:** [ppda.mw](https://ppda.mw/)
- **Mandate:** Regulation, monitoring, oversight, and enforcement of public procurement and disposal of assets
- **Key functions:** Set thresholds, approve non-standard methods, publish contract awards, maintain supplier registry, conduct audits, debar suppliers
- The PPDA publishes contract awards above **MK 50 million** (~US$29,000) on its website.

### Key Provisions of the 2025 Act

1. **Electronic Government Procurement (e-GP):** Mandates use of MANEPS for all government procurement.
2. **Personal liability for price inflation:** Officers transacting with grossly inflated prices face disciplinary action including dismissal.
3. **Bid evaluation timeline:** Must commence within **7 days** of bid opening.
4. **Single-source procurement:** Requires clearance from the **Anti-Corruption Bureau (ACB)** before contract signing -- a major anti-corruption provision.
5. **Beneficial ownership disclosure:** Mandatory transparency on entity ownership to prevent monopolization.
6. **Pricing controls:** Stricter mechanisms against inflated costs.
7. **Sustainable procurement:** New environmental/social requirements.

## 3. Government Tenders (Domestic Budget)

### Procurement Methods

The Act prescribes **seven methods** of procurement, each with conditions and thresholds:

| Method | When Used | Min. Bid Period |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Open Tender** (default) | All procurement unless exception applies | 30 days (national), 45 days (international) |
| **Restricted Tender** | Limited supplier availability; disproportionate cost of evaluating many bids | 21 days |
| **Two-Stage Tender** | Complex procurements; full technical specs unavailable initially | Varies |
| **International Competitive Bidding (ICB)** | Value exceeds threshold; goods unavailable domestically from 2+ suppliers; national tender unsuccessful | 45 days |
| **Request for Proposals (RFP)** | Consultancy services and intellectual services only | 21 days |
| **Request for Quotations (RFQ)** | Standard goods, small works, routine services below threshold | 5 days |
| **Single-Source Procurement** | Only one capable supplier; exclusive rights; emergency; compatibility | 5 days (24 hrs for emergency) |

### Procurement Thresholds

**Critical note:** Specific monetary thresholds are set by PPDA regulation (not in the Act itself) and are revised periodically. The most recent revisions:

- Thresholds effective **July 1, 2023**
- Further revision effective **April 1, 2024**

The thresholds determine which procurement method must be used based on estimated contract value. They are published as PPDA Circulars and are available at [ppda.mw/circulars](https://www.ppda.mw/circulars).

**What we know about threshold ranges (from supplier registration fees, which mirror value categories):**

| Category | Contract Value Range |
|----------|---------------------|
| 1 | Up to MK 10 million (~US$5,800) |
| 2 | MK 10M - MK 30M (~US$5,800-17,500) |
| 3 | MK 30M - MK 80M (~US$17,500-46,500) |
| 4 | MK 80M - MK 100M (~US$46,500-58,000) |
| 5 | MK 100M - MK 500M (~US$58,000-290,000) |
| 6 | MK 500M - MK 1 billion (~US$290,000-580,000) |
| 7 | Above MK 1 billion (~US$580,000+) |

| 1 | Up to K10M | K12,000 | -- |

### The Full Government Tender Process

**Step-by-step timeline (typical open tender):**

1. **Procurement Planning** (Pre-fiscal year)
   - Entity prepares Annual Procurement Plan
   - Since 2025/26, plans must be uploaded to MANEPS

2. **Tender Advertisement** (Day 0)
   - Published in **two widely circulated national newspapers**
   - Published on **PPDA website** (ppda.mw/tenders)
   - Displayed at entity's head office (publicly accessible)
   - For international tenders: international media in English
   - Gazette publication per PPDA prescriptions

3. **Bid Submission Period**
   - Open tender: minimum **30 days** (national) / **45 days** (international)
   - RFQ: minimum **5 days**
   - Restricted: minimum **21 days**

4. **Bid Opening** (Day 30+)
   - Public opening of bids

5. **Bid Evaluation** (Must start within 7 days of opening, per 2025 Act)
   - Lowest evaluated bidder wins (for goods/works)
   - Non-price criteria must be objective and quantifiable
   - Domestic/regional preference margins may apply
   - No price negotiations permitted (except under specific sections)

6. **Intention to Award** (14-day standstill)
   - Published in **two widely circulated newspapers** and on **PPDA website**
   - **14 calendar days** before contract can be signed
   - Applies to contracts above a threshold set by the Director General
   - Bidders may request debriefing or file complaints during this period

7. **Complaint/Review Period**
   - Written complaint within **14 days** of awareness
   - Proceedings suspended for **10 days** (extendable to 30)
   - Decision within **14 days**
   - Appeal to Director General, then to Review Committee, then to High Court

8. **Contract Signing**
   - Winning bidder must sign within **28 days** of receiving the contract
   - Performance security required before contract entry

9. **Contract Execution & Payment**
   - Procurement tied to quarterly Treasury allocations (per 2025/26 austerity circular)
   - Payment delays are endemic (see Section 9)

**Typical total duration: 3-6 months** from advertisement to contract signing for open tender, though delays frequently push this to 6-12 months or longer.

### Required Documentation for Bidders

- PPDA supplier registration certificate
- Tax clearance certificate (from MRA)
- Business registration certificate
- Financial statements (audited for larger contracts)
- Technical capability evidence
- Past performance references
- Bid security/bond (for larger tenders)
- Power of attorney for authorized signatory

### Record Retention

All procurement records must be retained for **5 years** from completion, termination, cancellation, or dispute resolution.

## 4. NGO / International Organization Tenders

### Key Principle

International organizations operating in Malawi generally follow **their own procurement guidelines**, not PPDA rules. They may use national procedures for small/local purchases, but major procurements follow their institutional frameworks.

### Organization-by-Organization Breakdown

#### World Bank

- **Framework:** World Bank Procurement Regulations for IPF Borrowers (July 2016)
- **System:** STEP (Systematic Tracking and Exchanges in Procurement) for plan preparation and clearance
- **Standard Documents:** Bank's Standard Procurement Documents for all international competitive contracts
- **National Procurement:** May use Malawi's own procedures for national-market purchases as specified in procurement plans
- **Anti-Corruption:** Mandatory compliance with Bank's Anti-Corruption Guidelines; right to sanction, inspect, and audit
- **Current Major Project:** Digital Malawi Program -- US$150 million total (US$70M Phase I + US$80M Phase II via IDA/PforR). The PPDA's e-GP and e-GM systems are funded under this program.
- **Publication:** [projects.worldbank.org](https://projects.worldbank.org) procurement section
- **Key difference from government:** Faster payment, stricter evaluation, international competition, higher compliance standards

#### African Development Bank (AfDB)

- **Framework:** AfDB Procurement Policy for Bank Group-Funded Operations
- **Principle:** Most open competitive procedures possible
- **Eligibility:** Equal opportunities for contractors, suppliers, and consultants from all 81 member countries (54 African + 27 non-African)
- **Publication:** [afdb.org/en/documents/category/specific-procurement-notices](https://www.afdb.org/en/documents/category/specific-procurement-notices)
- **Current Malawi projects:** Include infrastructure, agriculture, and digital transformation

#### United Nations System (UNDP, UNICEF, WFP, WHO)

- **Framework:** UN Procurement Manual; each agency has specific procedures
- **Central Portal:** [United Nations Global Marketplace (UNGM)](https://www.ungm.org/) -- 500,000+ registered suppliers globally
- **UNDP Malawi:** Publishes opportunities at [undp.org/malawi/procurement](https://www.undp.org/malawi/procurement)
- **UNICEF Malawi:** Publishes at [unicef.org/malawi/procurement-notices](https://www.unicef.org/malawi/procurement-notices)
- **WFP Malawi:**
  - Country Strategic Plan 2024-2028
  - Procured **33,000 MT of maize** + 317 MT fortified corn-soya blend for El Nino response
  - Reached **1.6 million people** in 2024-2025 lean season
  - Significant local food procurement -- exploring making Malawi a regional sourcing hub
  - Upgrading ADMARC weighbridges for procurement integration
- **Registration:** Suppliers must register on UNGM (Basic, Level 1, or Level 2 depending on contract value)
- **Supplier Code of Conduct:** Mandatory (labour, human rights, environment, ethics)

#### USAID

- **Framework:** Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) + USAID Acquisition Regulation (AIDAR) for contracts; 2 CFR 200 for grants
- **Scale in Malawi:** Over **US$350 million annually** across HIV/AIDS, health, agriculture, and education (pre-2025 cuts)
- **Major Program:** Global Health Supply Chain Program (GHSC-PSM) -- procurement and distribution to 650+ public health facilities
- **Publication:** [sam.gov](https://sam.gov) (contracts), [grants.gov](https://grants.gov) (grants)
- **Key difference:** US-specific regulations, often requires US-based prime contractors, local subcontracting encouraged
- **Recent disruption:** Malawi struggling to fill development gaps after US aid cuts (2025)

#### FCDO (UK - formerly DFID)

- **Framework:** UK Government Commercial Framework
- **Scale in Malawi:** Major programs include:
  - Climate resilience: up to GBP 90.5 million over 8 years (2018-2027), reaching 1.7 million people
  - Trade and investment program
  - Health Partnership Programme
- **Publication:** [Find a Tender (UK)](https://www.find-tender.service.gov.uk/), [DevTracker](https://devtracker.fcdo.gov.uk/countries/MW/projects)

#### European Union

- **Framework:** EU Financial Regulation procurement rules
- **Publication:** [TED (Tenders Electronic Daily)](https://ted.europa.eu/) for EU-funded external action contracts
- **Key difference:** Often uses contribution agreements through implementing partners (UN agencies, NGOs)

### Typical Value Ranges for Donor Tenders

| Type | Typical Range | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| Small local purchase | US$5,000-50,000 | Often direct/quotation-based |
| National competitive | US$50,000-500,000 | Published locally and on agency portals |
| International competitive | US$500,000+ | Published internationally |
| Large infrastructure (WB/AfDB) | US$1M-50M+ | Full ICB with prequalification |
| Consultancy services | US$10,000-5M+ | Quality-based selection common |

### Key Differences from Government Tenders

| Aspect | Government | Donor/NGO |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Rules | PPDA Act 2025 | Agency's own procurement policy |
| Payment speed | Slow (months to years) | Generally faster (30-60 days) |
| Transparency | Improving via MANEPS | Generally higher; published online |
| Corruption risk | Higher | Lower (stricter oversight) |
| Documentation | MWK-denominated | Often USD/EUR-denominated |
| Appeals | PPDA review mechanism | Agency-specific protest procedures |
| Local preference | Possible (domestic margin) | Limited; open to member countries |

## 5. Parastatal Tenders

### PPDA Oversight of Parastatals

All parastatals (statutory corporations, state-owned enterprises) fall under PPDA oversight. They are classified as Procuring and Disposing Entities (PDEs) and must follow the PPDA Act 2025.

### Major Parastatals and Their Procurement

#### ESCOM (Electricity Supply Corporation of Malawi)

- **Website:** [escom.mw/tenders](https://www.escom.mw/tenders/)
- **Core business:** Procurement, transmission, and distribution of electric power; System Market Operator and Single Buyer
- **Procurement profile:** Large infrastructure contracts (power generation, transmission lines, transformers), fuel supply, maintenance
- **Scandals:**
  - K5 billion fuel supply scam with NOCMA (2017-2018): NOCMA forged agreements, billed ESCOM for fuel that was never delivered
  - K57 billion gensets deal with Aggreko under ACB investigation
  - 2 million litres of diesel (K2.9 billion) went missing from NOCMA supply to ESCOM sites
  - Parliament's Public Accounts Committee red-flagged ESCOM as a "problem child" parastatal

#### NOCMA (National Oil Company of Malawi)

- **Procurement profile:** Fuel procurement (strategic reserves, distribution)
- **Scandals:**
  - K5 billion ESCOM fuel scam
  - "Dieselgate" -- 2 million litres of diesel (K3 billion) stolen
  - March 2026: US$25 million single-source contract to Savari Energy DMCC (alleged links to sanctioned businessman Zuneth Sattar) -- under ACB investigation
- **PPDA oversight:** Subject to procurement law but repeatedly found in violation

#### ADMARC (Agricultural Development and Marketing Corporation)

- **Procurement profile:** Agricultural inputs, storage facilities, IT equipment
- **Recent tenders:** Supply of desktop and laptop computers (February 2026, ref ADMARC/NCB/ICT/2025/2026/17)
- **WFP integration:** Weighbridges being upgraded by WFP for food procurement programs

#### MRA (Malawi Revenue Authority)

- **Website:** [mra.mw/tenders](https://www.mra.mw/tenders)
- **Procurement profile:** IT systems, vehicles, office equipment, customs infrastructure
- **Note:** MRA collects revenue (K1.539 trillion in 2022/23) but also procures as a PDE

#### MACRA (Malawi Communications Regulatory Authority)

- **Procurement profile:** Telecommunications regulatory equipment, IT systems, consulting services
- **Falls under PPDA:** Subject to same procurement rules

#### Water Boards (Lilongwe, Blantyre, Southern Region, Central Region, Northern Region)

- **Procurement profile:** Water infrastructure, pipes, pumps, treatment chemicals, civil works
- **PPDA oversight:** Listed alongside ESCOM and NOCMA as entities where supply cartels must be curtailed
- **Donor funding:** Many water projects are World Bank or AfDB funded, meaning dual oversight (PPDA + donor)

#### Other Notable Parastatals

- **MERA (Malawi Energy Regulatory Authority)** -- implicated in K5 billion ESCOM/NOCMA scam
- **EGENCO (Electricity Generation Company)** -- K6.7 billion in irregularities uncovered by auditors
- **Central Medical Stores (CMS)** -- major pharmaceutical procurement
- **MDF (Malawi Defence Force)** -- defense procurement (often exempt from full PPDA disclosure)

### Key Pattern for Parastatals

Parastatals are legally under PPDA but **enforcement is weak**. The largest procurement scandals in Malawi (ESCOM fuel scam, NOCMA diesel theft, EGENCO irregularities) all involve parastatals where oversight failed. The 2025 Act's requirement for ACB clearance on single-source contracts is a direct response to parastatal abuse.

## 6. The Actual Flow: Funding to Payment

### Flow A: Government Domestic Budget Tenders

```
Treasury (MoF) allocates budget
    |
    v
Ministry/Department/Council receives quarterly allocation
    |
    v
Internal Procurement & Disposal Committee (IPDC) approves procurement plan
    |
    v
Procurement Unit prepares bidding documents
    |
    v
Tender advertised: 2 newspapers + PPDA website + entity notice board
    |
    v
Bid submission period: 30 days (national) / 45 days (international)
    |
    v
Public bid opening
    |
    v
Bid evaluation (must start within 7 days of opening)
    |
    v
IPDC reviews evaluation report and recommends award
    |
    v
Intention to Award published: 2 newspapers + PPDA website (14-day standstill)
    |
    v
Contract signed (within 28 days of issuance)
    |
    v
Performance security deposited
    |
    v
Contract execution
    |
    v
Invoice submitted
    |
    v
Payment processed via IFMIS (Integrated Financial Management Information System)
    |
    v
Treasury releases funds (subject to quarterly cash allocation -- DELAYS COMMON)
```

**Who can bid:** Any supplier registered with PPDA (mandatory registration). Must have tax clearance from MRA.

**Payment timeline:** Officially 30 days from invoice, but in practice **3-12+ months** due to Treasury cash flow constraints.

### Flow B: Donor-Funded Tenders (World Bank example)

```
World Bank approves project and credit/grant (IDA)
    |
    v
Project Implementation Unit (PIU) in relevant Ministry prepares Procurement Plan
    |
    v
World Bank reviews and gives "No Objection" on procurement plan
    |
    v
Tender advertised: World Bank STEP system + local newspapers + international media (for ICB)
    |
    v
Bid submission: 45 days (ICB) or as per plan
    |
    v
Evaluation by PIU technical committee
    |
    v
World Bank "No Objection" on evaluation report (for prior review contracts)
    |
    v
Contract award and signing
    |
    v
Contract execution with World Bank supervision
    |
    v
Payment from designated account (funded by WB disbursement)
    |
    v
Payment typically within 30-60 days of certified invoice
```

**Who can bid:** Suppliers from World Bank member countries. No PPDA registration required, but local procurement may follow national procedures.

**Payment timeline:** Generally **30-60 days** -- much faster than domestic budget because funds are ring-fenced.

### Flow C: UN Agency Tenders (WFP/UNDP/UNICEF)

```
Agency HQ approves country program/budget
    |
    v
Country office identifies procurement need
    |
    v
Published on UNGM (+ agency website + sometimes local newspapers)
    |
    v
Bids received electronically via UNGM or agency e-submission system
    |
    v
Evaluation by agency procurement committee
    |
    v
Award (may require HQ approval for high-value contracts)
    |
    v
Contract signed
    |
    v
Execution under agency supervision
    |
    v
Payment from agency funds (30-45 days typical)
```

**Who can bid:** Must be registered on UNGM. Open to suppliers from all countries. Some opportunities specifically target local suppliers.

### Flow D: Parastatal Tenders

```
Parastatal Board approves budget/procurement plan
    |
    v
Internal Procurement Committee approves specific procurement
    |
    v
Advertised: parastatal website + newspapers + PPDA website
    |
    v
Same process as government (30-day bid period, evaluation, standstill)
    |
    v
Contract signed
    |
    v
Payment from parastatal's own revenue or government subvention
    |
    v
Payment timeline varies: 30-90 days (revenue-generating parastatals) to months (subvented ones)
```

**Key risk:** Parastatals are where procurement scandals concentrate. Single-source procurement has been heavily abused.

## 7. Newspaper Tenders

### Legal Requirement

The PPDA Act 2017 (Section 40) -- and continued in the 2025 Act -- requires that tenders be published in:

1. **Two widely circulated national newspapers**
2. **The PPDA website**
3. **The entity's head office notice board** (publicly accessible)

For international tenders, additional publication in international English-language media is required.

### The Newspaper Landscape

| Newspaper | Type | Circulation | Tender Significance |
|-----------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **The Nation** | Daily (Mon-Fri) | Largest private daily | Major tender publication outlet |
| **The Daily Times** | Daily | Oldest newspaper (est. 1895, Blantyre) | Major tender publication outlet |
| **Malawi News** | Weekend | Saturday publication | Some tenders |
| **The Weekend Nation** | Weekend | Saturday edition of The Nation | Some tenders |
| **Malawi Government Gazette** | Official | Government publication (since 1964) | Official legal notices, some tenders |

### How Significant Are Newspaper-Only Tenders?

**Very significant, and this is a key market opportunity.**

- The legal requirement for **two newspapers** means EVERY government and parastatal tender MUST appear in print.
- Many tenders, especially from **district councils, smaller ministries, and local government**, appear ONLY in newspapers and NOT on the PPDA website. The PPDA website historically only publishes contracts above MK 50 million.
- Smaller tenders (RFQs, restricted tenders below threshold) may appear in one newspaper or only on notice boards.
- NGO and donor tenders may or may not appear in newspapers -- they often publish only on their own portals.

**Estimated distribution (approximate):**

| Publication Channel | Coverage |
|--------------------|----------|
| Newspapers + PPDA website | ~40-50% of all government tenders |
| Newspapers only (not on PPDA website) | ~30-40% of government tenders |
| PPDA website only | ~5-10% |
| Entity website/notice board only | ~5-10% |
| Donor portals (not in newspapers) | Most donor tenders |

**Key insight for TendersMW:** Scraping newspaper tender advertisements is essential. A huge portion of the Malawi tender market is only accessible through print. This is a major gap that MANEPS aims to close but has not yet.

## 8. MANEPS: E-Procurement Transition

### What Is MANEPS?

- **Full name:** Malawi National Electronic Procurement System
- **URL:** [maneps.mw](https://maneps.mw/)
- **Purpose:** Web-based system to manage the full lifecycle of public procurement: vendor registration, planning, requisition, tendering, and contract management
- **Funding:** World Bank Digital Malawi Program (US$80 million for Phase II)

### The April 2026 Mandate

- **Directive:** Ministry of Finance, Economic Planning and Decentralisation circular dated **February 27, 2026**
- **Effective date:** **April 1, 2026** -- ALL government PDEs must use MANEPS
- **What it means:** All government tender advertisements, bid submissions, evaluations, and contract management should go through the MANEPS platform

### Current Adoption (as of March 2026)

| Metric | Status |
|--------|--------|
| Pilot PDEs onboarded | **32 entities** |
| Pilot PDEs that uploaded procurement plans for 2025/26 FY | All 32 |
| Total government PDEs that should be on MANEPS | Estimated 200+ (ministries, depts, agencies, councils) |
| Adoption rate | ~16% (32 of ~200+) |
| Supplier registration | Available via MANEPS; full vendor database with status tracking |

### What Changes on April 1, 2026?

1. **All PDEs must publish tenders on MANEPS** -- not just newspapers
2. **Bid submission may move online** -- but this is uncertain for initial rollout
3. **Procurement plans must be uploaded** -- already happening for pilot PDEs
4. **Contract management** -- tracking through the system
5. **Vendor registration** -- centralized through MANEPS

### Reality Check

- **"Years after its rollout, a new deadline underlines uneven adoption across procuring entities, and the state's reliance on deadlines where compliance has not become routine."** -- Center for Investigative Journalism Malawi
- MANEPS has been in development for years. The April 2026 deadline is an enforcement push, not a new launch.
- **Capacity gaps** at district councils and smaller entities will likely mean slow adoption beyond the 32 pilot PDEs.
- **Internet connectivity** in rural districts remains a barrier.
- Newspaper publication will likely **continue alongside** MANEPS, not replace it, given the legal requirement and digital divide.

### OCDS (Open Contracting Data Standard)

- MANEPS includes an **OCDS portal** (developed during 2023-2025 implementation period)
- Goal: Machine-readable procurement data for real-time citizen oversight
- OCDS system completion was anticipated by **end of May 2025**
- If fully implemented, would provide API access to structured tender data -- directly useful for TendersMW

### Implications for TendersMW

- **Short term (2026-2027):** MANEPS will be a data source but will NOT have complete coverage. Newspaper scraping remains essential.
- **Medium term (2027-2028):** If MANEPS adoption reaches 80%+, it becomes the primary source. OCDS API could enable real-time data feeds.
- **Long term:** Full e-procurement means TendersMW must differentiate on alerts, analysis, and value-add rather than just aggregation.

## 9. Procurement Challenges

### 9.1 Late Payments

**This is the #1 pain point for Malawi contractors.**

- **~40% of contracts** in Education, Health, Agriculture, Roads, and Water Points infrastructure were not executed, mostly due to procuring entities failing to pay suppliers (MAPS Assessment, 2019).
- Payment delays of **3-12 months** are common for domestic budget contracts.
- The **M'mbelwa Office Complex** stalled since 2018: original contract K1.8 billion, contractor requested additional K9 billion due to inflation and delays.
- **MAREP 9** (Rural Electrification Programme): contractors reported financial challenges due to delayed payments.
- Procurement is tied to **quarterly Treasury allocations** -- if Treasury doesn't release funds, payments stop regardless of contract terms.
- Kwacha depreciation compounds the problem: a contract denominated in MWK loses real value during payment delays.

### 9.2 Corruption and Fraud

**Scale of the problem:**

- **Cashgate Scandal (2013):** MK 13.67 billion misappropriated in 6 months. Methods: payments without documentation, ghost workers/suppliers, diverted funds, inflated procurement. Baker Tilly audit found MK 6B+ in cashgate transactions, MK 4B in unsupported payments, MK 3.6B in inflated procurement. Western donors suspended ~$150 million/year in budget support. 70+ people arrested.
- **2022 Audit:** K1.466 trillion in irregularities and non-compliance -- nearly half the national budget for that year. Largest portion (K1.412 trillion) was bank reconciliation irregularities. Procurement violations included K2 billion spent through the Malawi Embassy in India without IPDC approval.
- **ESCOM/NOCMA K5 billion fuel scam (2017-2018):** Forged agreements, fuel never delivered but payment made.
- **EGENCO:** K6.7 billion in irregularities uncovered by auditors.
- **NOCMA US$25 million Sattar-linked contract (2026):** Single-source procurement to allegedly sanctioned businessman's company.
- **K1.5 trillion** in dubious fuel and fertilizer deals involving top government officials (2024).

**Procurement-specific corruption patterns:**

- Single-source procurement abuse (now requires ACB clearance under 2025 Act)
- Bid rigging and collusion (recognized governance problem; PPDA receives reports)
- Inflated pricing
- Payments for goods/services never delivered
- Conflict of interest violations (weak enforcement of disclosure requirements)

### 9.3 PPDA Enforcement Actions

**The PPDA is taking a tougher stance under the 2025 Act:**

- **Five companies debarred (2025):**
  - Mata Butchery (Mzuzu): **60 months** debarment
  - NTK Investments (Blantyre): **48 months** (fraudulent/unethical conduct in bidding)
  - DMC Investments and Consultants (Lilongwe): debarred
  - AG Auto Services: debarred
  - Frapat Investment (Zomba): debarred
- Debarment periods range from **6 to 60 months** depending on severity.
- Debarred supplier list maintained on MANEPS.
- However, enforcement capacity remains limited relative to the scale of violations.

### 9.4 Capacity Issues

- **Insufficient staffing** at PPDA and across procuring entities
- **Lack of specialized training** for procurement officers, especially at district level
- **Limited financial resources** for the PPDA itself
- **Inconsistencies** between procurement laws and broader financial management policies
- **No independent procurement appeals body** -- the 2019 MAPS Assessment recommended creating one
- **Weak whistleblower protection** -- recommended strengthening
- **No structured certification** or career development for procurement professionals

### 9.5 Market Competition Issues

- **Lack of access to finance** for local suppliers limits participation
- **Inconsistent application** of procurement rules by government agencies
- **Lack of information** on requirements and procedures
- **Difficulty obtaining bidding documents** -- pre-MANEPS, this meant physically visiting offices
- **Collusion and supply cartels** -- recognized problem especially for parastatals (Water Boards, ESCOM, NOCMA, ADMARC, MDF, Police)

### 9.6 Project Delays and Cost Overruns

- Projects taking **5-6 years** instead of planned timelines
- Design changes mid-project
- Currency depreciation (Kwacha has lost significant value)
- Building material cost inflation
- Political connections allegedly shielding contractors from accountability
- **Minister Ben Phiri (2025):** "These delays are costing government billions. Every day that passes means higher prices for cement, steel, and other building materials."

### 9.7 Transparency Rating

- The Transparent Public Procurement Rating ([tpp-rating.org](https://www.tpp-rating.org/page/eng/country/malawi)) assesses Malawi's procurement transparency.
- PPDA website publishes contract awards only above **MK 50 million** in PDF format.
- Many procurement processes remain opaque, fragmented, and vulnerable to political interference.

#### 1. MalawiTenders.com

- **Operator:** Global Tenders Services Pvt. Ltd. (Indian company)
- **URL:** [malawitenders.com](https://www.malawitenders.com/)

- **Coverage:** Government departments, local authorities, PSUs, private entities
- **Pricing:** Not publicly displayed; requires registration to view plans
- **Weakness:** Indian-operated, likely scraping PPDA/newspaper data. No local presence. Generic platform replicated across many countries.

#### 2. Ntchito.com

- **Operator:** Local Malawian platform
- **URL:** [ntchito.com](https://ntchito.com/)
- **Model:** Free (ad-supported / jobs-focused)
- **Content:** Tenders, jobs, internships, grants, scholarships, consultancies
- **Coverage:** Government, NGOs, donors, private firms across multiple sectors
- **Strength:** Local, trusted, broad user base, covers both jobs and tenders
- **Weakness:** Tenders are secondary to jobs; no premium features for serious bid-seekers; limited alert customization

#### 3. CareersMW.com

- **Operator:** Local Malawian platform
- **URL:** [careersmw.com](https://careersmw.com/)
- **Model:** Free (jobs-focused)
- **Content:** Tenders and non-consultancy services section
- **Coverage:** Accepts submissions from advertisers
- **Strength:** Local brand recognition
- **Weakness:** Jobs-first platform; tenders are an afterthought; minimal tender-specific features

#### 4. PPDA Website (ppda.mw)

- **Type:** Government official portal
- **Content:** Tender notices, intention-to-award notices, award notices, supplier registration
- **Strength:** Official source; free; covers all PDEs (in theory)
- **Weakness:** Only publishes contracts above MK 50 million; PDF format; poor search; no alerts; incomplete coverage of smaller tenders; no historical data analysis

#### 5. MANEPS (maneps.mw)

- **Type:** Official government e-procurement platform
- **Status:** 32 pilot PDEs live; mandatory for all from April 2026
- **Strength:** Full lifecycle management; will be the official primary channel
- **Weakness:** Low adoption; capacity gaps; not a tender aggregation platform (it's a transactional system)
